# Market pain points and willingness-to-pay: secondary research for Matadora

This is customer-discovery-by-secondary-research, not primary user interviews. It looks for places where the Polish/EU construction industry already demonstrably pays real money to solve a documented problem, and rates how close each is to something Matadora already has.

**Strongest opportunity: payment delays and invoice factoring.** This angle has the hardest evidence of any of the six: multiple independent 2025-2026 surveys (Coface, Atradius, BIK) converge on construction as Poland's worst sector for late payment, with average delays near 59 days and billions of PLN in overdue construction-sector debt, and a whole industry of factoring firms already charges 0.3-4% of invoice value to solve exactly this. Matadora already holds the invoice, project-milestone, and counterparty-trust data a factor needs to underwrite, so the natural move is an embedded referral to a factoring partner rather than becoming a lender itself. Every other angle below has either weaker evidence, weaker willingness-to-pay proof, or both.

## Ranked opportunities

### 1. Payment delays / invoice factoring
Construction is Poland's worst-hit sector for late payment: Coface's 2026 Poland Payment Survey found average construction payment delays of 59 days (up from 46.2 in 2024), against a record national B2B average of 54.1 days, the worst since Coface started surveying in 2017. Atradius's 2025 Poland barometer put ~36% of construction invoices overdue, and BIK/BIG InfoMonitor reporting (via alebank.pl and inzynierbudownictwa.pl, the latter only confirmed via search snippet, not a full fetch) put overdue construction-sector debt at roughly 5.8-6 billion PLN by mid-2025, with 97% of firms reporting payment bottlenecks (that 97% figure is reported, not independently verified). The willingness-to-pay evidence is concrete and multi-sourced: general factoring runs 1.5-4% of invoice value; Bibby Financial Services' construction-specific offering (financing against payment certificates) charges 0.3-2.5%; reverse-factoring providers (INDOS, PKO Faktoring) price around 0.09%/day, roughly 4.2% for a 45-day advance. Matadora's finance module already has the invoicing, VAT, KSeF, and project-milestone data a factor needs to underwrite risk, but has no discounting engine, credit scoring, or licensed lending capability of its own. Revenue model: embed a "get paid now" button on invoices that routes to a partner factor via API, take a 10-20% referral cut of the factor's fee (or a flat bps override) plus a small platform processing fee; a later phase could add white-label reverse factoring for the wholesaler-contractor chain.

### 2. KSeF-adjacent compliance automation
KSeF e-invoicing is now mandatory, and Polish firms are visibly struggling: only about 12% of large/medium enterprises report being fully prepared, SME confidence in the need for KSeF fell from 61% to 52% over five months (SaldeoSMART survey via brandsit.pl), and the practical bite in 2026 is that an invoice issued outside KSeF has no legal effect, so the buyer simply loses the VAT deduction, no fine required. Construction has a specific structural problem here: KSeF only accepts structured XML with no attachments, so contractors can't attach photos, protocols, or contracts to an invoice, and risk misrouting invoices to the wrong project when pulling from the central system (develogic.pl, symfonia.pl). Willingness to pay for basic KSeF compliance itself has collapsed to near zero (inFakt, wFirma, Fakturownia bundle it free into base bookkeeping plans), but volume-tiered pricing still exists at Comarch (15-600 zł/month by invoice volume) and Symfonia (59 zł/year up to enterprise tiers), showing the market pays for KSeF tooling that scales with complexity. Matadora's own `src/lib/ksef/generate-xml.ts` only builds FA(2) XML client-side; it does not do live API submission, session/certificate auth, or receiving invoices, so it is currently behind even the free-tier competitors. The defensible premium is not "KSeF compliance" (commoditized) but auto-linking submitted/received KSeF invoices to `offer_stages`, `milestones`, and protocols, solving the "no attachments" problem specifically for multi-subcontractor general contractors. Revenue model: a paid tier feature at roughly 50-150 zł/project/month, priced on number of linked subcontractor entities rather than invoice count.

### 3. Subcontractor / bid management chaos
Direct quantification of time lost to manual bid and subcontractor management is thin. A widely repeated "70% of bids fail" statistic has no traceable source and should be treated as vendor marketing, not evidence. The closest credible number is Autodesk/FMI's "Construction Disconnected" report: construction professionals spend about 35% of their time (14+ hours/week) on non-productive activities including searching for project information and conflict resolution, a broader category than bid management alone. Where the evidence is genuinely strong is willingness to pay: BuildingConnected (Autodesk) is reported charging $3,600-$5,000+/year depending on contractor revenue, and Procore's pricing (tied to Annual Construction Volume) produces real customer bills of roughly $15,000-$55,000/year for bid-board and prequalification tooling. This is the angle closest to what Matadora already ships: the marketplace bidding loop (investor publishes, contractors bid, investor accepts) and the subcontractor/RFQ/purchase-order tables already cover the core workflow that BuildingConnected and Procore Bid Management charge for, so re-selling that loop is not incremental. The genuinely unbuilt piece is automated counterparty risk scoring (KRS/CEIDG status, VAT/ZUS payment history, insurance validity, a Polish analogue to Autodesk's TradeTapp) and payment-guarantee/escrow between investor-contractor-subcontractor tiers. Revenue model: a per-check or subscription "subcontractor verification" add-on (roughly 20-50 PLN/check or 99-299 PLN/month per active project), optionally paired with a 0.5-1% payment-guarantee fee on subcontract value.

### 4. Disputes and change-order documentation
Global figures are large but not Poland-specific: Arcadis's 2025 Global Construction Disputes Report found average US dispute value of $60.1M; HKA's CRUX Insight (2,200+ projects, 114 countries) found disputed sums averaging 33.4% of contract budgets, with incorrect design as Europe's top disruption cause (32.3% of projects affected). The best Poland-specific source, CAS's annual construction disputes report, is sentiment rather than a cost benchmark: 44% of respondents expect dispute values to keep rising in 2025, "wait and see" (65%) and litigation (57%) dominate over mediation (12%) or arbitration (7%), and courts can take years versus 3-6 months for arbitration. The most concrete forward-looking fact is regulatory: Poland is introducing mandatory pre-litigation mediation for construction disputes from March 2026, a real tailwind for documentation tooling. Willingness-to-pay evidence is weaker here: no dedicated claims-management product publishes Poland pricing (Payapps and Autodesk Construction Cloud are contact-sales only); the closest comparable is PlanRadar's general per-seat construction-documentation pricing ($32-239/user/month), which is adjacent, not dispute-specific. Matadora already has real infrastructure that's closer to dispute-avoidance than a bare tracker: `change_orders`, `rfis` with cost/schedule impact fields, versioned `project_documents`, `daily_site_reports`, and `project_photos`. What's missing is tamper-evident timestamping of evidence, schedule-linked delay analysis, and an exportable "claim bundle" for mediators, which the March 2026 mediation mandate makes newly relevant. Revenue model: a per-project add-on (200-500 PLN/month) bundling notarized photo/log timestamping and auto-generated, mediation-ready claim packets, upsold to existing seats.

### 5. Cost and schedule overruns
The overrun statistics are large and well cited but mostly not Poland-specific, and some figures could not be independently verified. McKinsey's "Construction Productivity Imperative" is widely cited for 98% of megaprojects running over 30% cost overrun and 77% running at least 40% late; a newer ~79% average cost overrun figure is circulating in 2025-2026 secondary sources but could not be confirmed directly against a McKinsey original (fetch attempts were blocked). KPMG's Global Construction Survey (again only accessible via secondary summaries, original PDF blocked) suggests only 25-31% of projects land within 10% of original budget. No Poland-specific overrun percentage was found. On pricing, PlanRadar (roughly $49-239/user/month, triangulated across aggregator sites since planradar.com itself returned 403) and Autodesk Build ($39-139/user/month per its own pricing page) are the only vendors with genuinely published rather than "contact sales" prices in this category. Matadora already has more scaffolding here than expected: migration `0024_pricebook_evm_drawings.sql` defines a full `evm_snapshots` table with generated SV/CV/SPI/CPI/ETC/EAC columns, and `src/lib/actions/evm.ts` exposes snapshot creation, but planned value, earned value, and actual cost are all manually entered rather than derived automatically from `offer_stages`, `milestones`, or `invoices`/`purchase_orders`. This is a build-out of existing scaffolding, not a new module: automating PV/EV/AC derivation and adding variance alerting would make it a real product. Revenue model: a per-project add-on (200-500 PLN/month) for automated EVM/variance tracking, priced below PlanRadar's per-seat floor.

### 6. Insurance, warranty, and liability
This is the weakest angle on both axes and should be treated as low priority. The best pain evidence is general rework cost, not warranty claims specifically: CII's IR-153 study puts direct field rework at about 5% of project cost (90th percentile 12.4%), and the UK's Get It Right Initiative estimates 10-25% of UK construction cost is lost to avoidable error. No Poland-specific statistic on defect rates, warranty-claim frequency, or claim cost was found despite targeted searches; Polish sources only explain the statutory rękojmia/gwarancja mechanics (2-year default, extendable to 5-10 years), with no hard numbers attached. Willingness-to-pay evidence is essentially absent: no vendor sells a distinct "warranty/liability-reduction" product with public pricing, and one industry paper on BIM and professional indemnity insurance explicitly argues that better documentation "should not materially alter risk profile," undercutting the premium-discount narrative some vendors imply. Matadora already has a real warranty module (`src/lib/actions/warranty.ts`, `/dashboard/gwarancje`, migration `0020_warranty_notifications_tasks.sql`) covering claim lifecycle, categories, and expiry alerts, ahead of where the market evidence would justify further investment. Given the near-total absence of Poland-specific pain data or willingness-to-pay proof, this reads better as a retention feature bundled into the core subscription than as a chargeable module, and further build-out here is not supported by the evidence gathered.
